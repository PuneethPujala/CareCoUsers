## Drug: Paracetamol

**Primary Use**: Analgesic (pain relief) and antipyretic (fever reduction). First-line treatment for mild-to-moderate pain, fever, headache, myalgia, and post-vaccination fever in adults and children.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Oral: 500 mg to 1000 mg per dose
- Maximum single dose: 1000 mg
- Maximum daily dose: 4000 mg (4 g) in healthy adults
- Maximum daily dose (liver disease / chronic alcohol use): 2000 mg (2 g)
- Dosing interval: Every 4–6 hours as needed

**When to Take**:
- Can be taken with or without food
- Post-meals preferred if gastric sensitivity is present
- For fever: Take at first sign of temperature >38.5°C (101.3°F)

**Common Side Effects**:
- Nausea (uncommon at therapeutic doses)
- Skin rash or urticaria (rare)
- Elevated liver enzymes with prolonged use
- Mild abdominal discomfort

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Overdose is the leading cause of acute liver failure in India. Doses >7.5–10 g in a single event can cause fatal liver damage.
- **Do NOT exceed 4 g/day** under any circumstance in healthy adults.
- **Alcohol interaction**: Concurrent chronic alcohol use with Paracetamol causes severe hepatotoxicity at even normal doses.
- Seek emergency care immediately for: right upper quadrant pain, jaundice, dark urine, confusion — these may indicate liver failure post-overdose.
- **Overdose antidote**: N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) — must be administered within 8–10 hours of overdose for maximum efficacy.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Warfarin**: Paracetamol at >2 g/day for >1 week significantly potentiates anticoagulant effect; increases bleeding risk.
- **Isoniazid (TB drug)**: Dramatically increases hepatotoxic risk; combination requires strict monitoring.
- **Carbamazepine / Phenytoin / Phenobarbitone**: These enzyme inducers accelerate Paracetamol metabolism into toxic metabolites, increasing liver damage risk.
- **Alcohol (chronic use)**: Severe hepatotoxicity risk even at standard doses.
- **Cholestyramine**: Reduces Paracetamol absorption if taken simultaneously; separate by 1 hour.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Generally considered safe at lowest effective dose for shortest duration. Recent studies suggest possible association with neurodevelopmental effects with prolonged use — consult obstetrician.
- **Lactation**: Safe. Excreted in breast milk in small amounts; no adverse effects reported in nursing infants.

## Drug: Cetirizine

**Primary Use**: Second-generation H1 antihistamine. Treatment of allergic rhinitis (seasonal and perennial), urticaria (hives), atopic dermatitis, allergic conjunctivitis, and insect bite reactions.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Oral: 10 mg once daily
- For elderly or renal impairment (CrCl 11–31 mL/min): 5 mg once daily
- Children 6–12 years: 5 mg once or twice daily
- Children 2–6 years: 2.5 mg once or twice daily

**When to Take**:
- Can be taken with or without food
- Preferably at night — mild sedation helps with nocturnal allergy symptoms
- Consistent daily timing recommended for chronic allergy management

**Common Side Effects**:
- Somnolence/drowsiness (most common; affects ~10–15% of users)
- Dry mouth (xerostomia)
- Headache
- Fatigue
- Mild dizziness

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Driving/Operating Machinery**: Despite being "non-drowsy" compared to first-generation antihistamines, Cetirizine causes drowsiness in a significant minority. Patients should assess individual response before driving.
- **Renal Impairment**: Cetirizine is renally excreted. Dose reduction is mandatory for patients with CrCl <50 mL/min. Risk of drug accumulation and CNS toxicity.
- **Severe renal failure (CrCl <10 mL/min)**: Cetirizine is contraindicated.
- QT prolongation risk in overdose — seek emergency care.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **CNS depressants (Benzodiazepines, Opioids, Alcohol)**: Additive sedation; can impair respiratory drive.
- **Theophylline (high dose)**: May reduce Cetirizine clearance, increasing plasma levels.
- **Ritonavir**: Increases Cetirizine exposure, worsening CNS side effects.
- **MAO Inhibitors**: Contraindicated; risk of hypertensive crisis and CNS toxicity.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Category B (US FDA). Generally considered acceptable in 2nd and 3rd trimesters. Avoid in 1st trimester unless benefit clearly outweighs risk. Consult doctor.
- **Lactation**: Not recommended. Excreted in breast milk; may cause sedation in nursing infant.

## Drug: Metformin

**Primary Use**: First-line oral antidiabetic drug for Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM). Reduces hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis), improves peripheral insulin sensitivity, and reduces intestinal glucose absorption. Also used in Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS).

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Starting dose: 500 mg once or twice daily with meals (to reduce GI side effects)
- Maintenance: 1000 mg twice daily (most common regimen in India)
- Maximum dose: 2500–3000 mg/day (divided doses)
- Extended-release (Metformin SR/XR): 500–2000 mg once daily with evening meal

**When to Take**:
- **Strictly with meals or immediately after food** — this is non-negotiable to reduce GI intolerance
- Extended-release: With the largest meal of the day (typically dinner in Indian context)
- Do NOT take on an empty stomach

**Common Side Effects**:
- Nausea and vomiting (very common, especially on initiation)
- Diarrhea and loose stools
- Abdominal bloating and flatulence
- Metallic taste in mouth
- Vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use (>3–5 years) — annual B12 monitoring recommended

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Lactic Acidosis**: Rare but potentially fatal complication. Risk increases with: renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²), hepatic failure, excessive alcohol intake, dehydration, contrast dye procedures.
- **Hold Metformin 48 hours before and after IV iodinated contrast** (e.g., CT scans with contrast, angiography) — risk of contrast-induced nephropathy leading to lactic acidosis.
- **Contraindicated** in: eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m², active liver disease, acute or chronic metabolic acidosis, diabetic ketoacidosis.
- **Symptoms of lactic acidosis** (EMERGENCY): Severe muscle pain, difficulty breathing, stomach pain, nausea/vomiting, dizziness, irregular heartbeat. Go to emergency immediately.
- Metformin does NOT cause hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy.
- Withhold during acute illness causing dehydration (vomiting, fever, diarrhea).

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Alcohol**: Potentiates lactic acidosis risk. Heavy drinking is contraindicated.
- **Iodinated contrast agents**: Must hold Metformin 48 hours before and after. Resume only after confirming renal function is stable.
- **Topiramate / Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors**: Increase risk of metabolic acidosis and lactic acidosis.
- **Cimetidine**: Reduces renal tubular secretion of Metformin, increasing plasma levels significantly.
- **Diuretics (Furosemide, Thiazides)**: Risk of dehydration → reduced renal perfusion → increased lactic acidosis risk.
- **NSAIDs (Diclofenac, Ibuprofen)**: Chronic use can impair renal function, contraindicated in combination with Metformin in renal risk patients.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Increasingly used in gestational diabetes and PCOS; however, Insulin remains the gold standard in India for gestational diabetes. Use only under specialist supervision.
- **Lactation**: Limited data. Generally avoided; consult endocrinologist.

## Drug: Pantoprazole

**Primary Use**: Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI). Treatment of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), peptic ulcer disease (gastric and duodenal ulcers), Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, H. pylori eradication (as part of triple therapy), and NSAID-induced gastropathy.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- GERD (mild-moderate): 20–40 mg once daily
- Peptic ulcer / H. pylori eradication: 40 mg twice daily (as part of triple therapy with Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin)
- Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: 80–240 mg/day in divided doses
- IV (hospital use): 40–80 mg once or twice daily

**When to Take**:
- **30–60 minutes BEFORE the first meal of the day (empty stomach)** — critical for maximum efficacy
- The drug must be activated in the acid environment of the stomach; food intake timing affects bioavailability
- For twice-daily dosing: 30–60 minutes before breakfast AND dinner

**Common Side Effects**:
- Headache
- Diarrhea or constipation
- Nausea
- Flatulence and abdominal pain
- Elevated liver enzymes (rare, with long-term use)

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Long-term PPI use (>1 year)** is associated with: Hypomagnesaemia (low magnesium), Hypocalcaemia, Vitamin B12 deficiency, increased risk of C. difficile infection, increased hip/spine fracture risk (due to reduced calcium absorption), and Community-acquired pneumonia.
- **Do NOT use long-term without a valid clinical indication** — widespread OTC overuse of PPIs is a significant concern in India.
- **Interstitial nephritis**: Rare but serious. Symptoms: decreased urine output, blood in urine, fever. Seek emergency care.
- **Atrophic gastritis** with long-term H. pylori-positive patients on PPI — periodic endoscopy monitoring recommended.
- **Rebound acid hypersecretion** can occur on abrupt discontinuation after prolonged use; taper if needed.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Clopidogrel**: Pantoprazole (and other PPIs, particularly Omeprazole) reduce activation of Clopidogrel by inhibiting CYP2C19. Increased risk of cardiovascular events in patients on dual antiplatelet therapy. Rabeprazole or Pantoprazole preferred over Omeprazole in this scenario.
- **Methotrexate**: PPIs reduce renal clearance of Methotrexate, increasing toxicity risk.
- **Atazanavir / Nelfinavir (HIV antivirals)**: PPIs reduce absorption; combination contraindicated or requires dose adjustment.
- **Ketoconazole / Itraconazole / Posaconazole**: Require acidic environment for absorption; PPIs significantly reduce antifungal plasma levels.
- **Digoxin**: PPI-induced hypomagnesaemia can increase Digoxin toxicity.
- **Iron / Calcium supplements**: Reduced absorption in alkaline gastric environment; separate dosing by ≥2 hours.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Generally considered safe (Category B). Used when GERD is severe and lifestyle modifications insufficient. Preferred over H2 blockers in some contexts.
- **Lactation**: Limited data. Excreted in breast milk. Use with caution; consult gastroenterologist.

## Drug: Amlodipine

**Primary Use**: Dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker (CCB). First-line treatment for hypertension (high blood pressure) and chronic stable angina pectoris. Also used for Prinzmetal's (vasospastic) angina.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Hypertension: Starting dose 5 mg once daily; may increase to 10 mg once daily after 7–14 days
- Angina: 5–10 mg once daily
- Elderly patients (>65 years): Start at 2.5 mg once daily
- Hepatic impairment: Start at 2.5 mg; titrate cautiously

**When to Take**:
- Once daily, at a fixed time each day
- Can be taken with or without food
- Morning dosing is most common in Indian clinical practice
- Do NOT stop abruptly — abrupt withdrawal can precipitate rebound angina

**Common Side Effects**:
- Peripheral oedema (ankle/leg swelling) — most common; affects ~10% of patients
- Flushing and facial warmth (vasodilation effect)
- Headache
- Palpitations
- Dizziness on standing (postural hypotension)
- Gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth) with long-term use

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Hypotension**: Significant blood pressure lowering, especially in combination with other antihypertensives or with dehydration. Monitor BP regularly, especially on initiation.
- **Aortic stenosis**: Use with extreme caution — vasodilation can precipitate cardiovascular collapse.
- **Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction**: Dihydropyridine CCBs (Amlodipine, Felodipine) are relatively safe, but Verapamil and Diltiazem (non-dihydropyridines) are contraindicated in HFrEF.
- **Reflex tachycardia**: Vasodilation can trigger compensatory tachycardia — monitor heart rate.
- **Liver disease**: Amlodipine is extensively hepatically metabolized; significant dose reduction needed in severe hepatic impairment.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Simvastatin**: Amlodipine inhibits CYP3A4, increasing Simvastatin plasma levels significantly. Risk of myopathy and rhabdomyolysis. Simvastatin dose should not exceed 20 mg/day with Amlodipine; prefer Atorvastatin or Rosuvastatin.
- **Cyclosporine / Tacrolimus**: Amlodipine increases plasma levels of these immunosuppressants.
- **Rifampicin**: Potent CYP3A4 inducer; reduces Amlodipine efficacy significantly.
- **Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (Ketoconazole, Itraconazole, Erythromycin, Clarithromycin)**: Increase Amlodipine levels; risk of severe hypotension.
- **Sildenafil / Tadalafil**: Additive hypotensive effect, especially dangerous in patients with cardiovascular disease.
- **Beta-blockers (Atenolol, Metoprolol)**: Generally safe combination for hypertension; however, risk of severe bradycardia if combined with non-dihydropyridine CCBs.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Category C. Generally avoided, especially in 1st trimester. Methyldopa or Labetalol preferred for hypertension in pregnancy in India. Amlodipine only if alternatives fail.
- **Lactation**: Avoid. Excreted in breast milk; effects on nursing infant unknown.

## Drug: Amoxicillin

**Primary Use**: Aminopenicillin antibiotic (broad-spectrum). Treatment of bacterial infections including: upper and lower respiratory tract infections (pharyngitis, tonsillitis, sinusitis, community-acquired pneumonia), urinary tract infections (UTIs), skin and soft tissue infections, H. pylori eradication (triple therapy), typhoid (in susceptible strains), and dental infections.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Mild-to-moderate infections: 250–500 mg every 8 hours (TID)
- Severe infections: 500–1000 mg every 8 hours
- H. pylori eradication: 1000 mg twice daily (with PPI + Clarithromycin or Metronidazole)
- Duration: 5–14 days depending on infection type; complete the full course

**When to Take**:
- Can be taken with or without food
- Post-meals reduces GI side effects (nausea)
- Take at evenly spaced intervals to maintain therapeutic blood levels (every 8 hours = every 8 hours, not "thrice daily as convenient")
- **Complete the full prescribed course** — do not stop early even if feeling better

**Common Side Effects**:
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhea (very common)
- Skin rash (maculopapular rash — may indicate allergy vs. drug rash in EBV patients)
- Oral thrush (Candidiasis) with prolonged use
- Abdominal pain

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Penicillin Allergy**: Amoxicillin is a penicillin. Patients with documented penicillin allergy must NOT receive Amoxicillin. Cross-reactivity with Cephalosporins exists (1–10%); use with caution.
- **Anaphylaxis** (EMERGENCY): Severe allergic reaction — urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension, collapse within minutes of dose. Requires immediate Adrenaline (Epinephrine) 0.5 mg IM. Call ambulance immediately.
- **Clostridioides (C. difficile) colitis**: Antibiotic-associated diarrhea that can progress to severe pseudomembranous colitis. Symptom: profuse watery or bloody diarrhea during or after antibiotic course. Seek immediate medical attention.
- **Amoxicillin rash in EBV (Infectious Mononucleosis)**: Patients with undiagnosed EBV/mono who receive Amoxicillin develop a widespread maculopapular rash — this is NOT a true allergy but indicates EBV infection.
- **Antibiotic resistance**: Do NOT use Amoxicillin for viral infections (cold, flu, COVID-19). Misuse contributes to antimicrobial resistance — a major public health crisis in India.
- Renal dose adjustment required for CrCl <30 mL/min.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Methotrexate**: Penicillins reduce renal clearance of Methotrexate; risk of severe toxicity (bone marrow suppression, mucositis).
- **Warfarin / Acenocoumarol**: Amoxicillin can alter gut flora, reducing Vitamin K synthesis, potentiating anticoagulant effect. INR monitoring required.
- **Oral Contraceptive Pills (OCPs)**: Historically debated; evidence suggests minimal interaction but some guidelines recommend backup contraception during and 7 days after antibiotic course.
- **Allopurinol**: Combined use significantly increases risk of skin rash.
- **Probenecid**: Reduces renal tubular secretion of Amoxicillin, increasing and prolonging blood levels (sometimes used intentionally).

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Category B. Considered safe throughout pregnancy. One of the preferred antibiotics in pregnancy for susceptible infections.
- **Lactation**: Safe. Excreted in breast milk in low concentrations; monitor infant for diarrhea or thrush.

## Drug: Telmisartan

**Primary Use**: Angiotensin II Receptor Blocker (ARB). Treatment of hypertension (high blood pressure), reduction of cardiovascular risk in patients with established atherosclerotic disease or Type 2 diabetes with target organ damage, and heart failure (as alternative to ACE inhibitors).

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Hypertension: 20–80 mg once daily
- Starting dose: 40 mg once daily
- Cardiovascular risk reduction: 80 mg once daily
- Maximum dose: 80 mg once daily

**When to Take**:
- Once daily, at same time each day
- Can be taken with or without food
- No specific food restrictions

**Common Side Effects**:
- Dizziness and light-headedness (especially on first dose — "first dose hypotension")
- Back pain and musculoskeletal pain
- Sinusitis
- Diarrhea
- Upper respiratory tract infections
- Hyperkalaemia (elevated potassium) — especially in renal impairment or combination with potassium-sparing diuretics

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Pregnancy**: Telmisartan is **CONTRAINDICATED** from 2nd trimester onwards. Causes fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios, neonatal renal failure, limb contractures, craniofacial deformities, and neonatal death. Use of ARBs in known pregnancy must be stopped immediately and doctor informed.
- **Bilateral renal artery stenosis**: ARBs can precipitate acute renal failure. Contraindicated.
- **Hyperkalaemia risk**: Elevated with renal impairment, diabetes, concurrent ACE inhibitor use, or potassium-sparing diuretics. Monitor potassium levels.
- **Acute kidney injury**: Risk during dehydration, fever, diarrhea — temporarily discontinue and consult doctor.
- **First-dose hypotension**: Particularly risk in volume-depleted patients (on diuretics, low-salt diet). Start low, monitor BP after first dose.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **ACE Inhibitors (Ramipril, Enalapril) + ARB combination**: Dual blockade increases risk of hypotension, hyperkalaemia, and acute renal failure. Generally contraindicated.
- **Aliskiren (Direct renin inhibitor)**: Triple RAAS blockade — contraindicated in diabetic patients.
- **Potassium-sparing diuretics (Spironolactone, Amiloride)**: Additive hyperkalaemia risk — life-threatening arrhythmias possible.
- **NSAIDs (Diclofenac, Ibuprofen)**: Reduce antihypertensive efficacy of ARBs; increase risk of acute renal failure in combination.
- **Lithium**: ARBs reduce renal lithium clearance; risk of lithium toxicity (tremors, confusion, renal damage).
- **Digoxin**: Telmisartan increases Digoxin plasma levels by ~20%; risk of Digoxin toxicity.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **CONTRAINDICATED** — especially from 2nd trimester. Stop immediately if pregnancy detected.
- **Lactation**: Avoid. Insufficient data; potential neonatal harm.

## Drug: Levothyroxine (Thyroxine / T4)

**Primary Use**: Synthetic thyroid hormone replacement. Treatment of hypothyroidism (primary, secondary, congenital), thyroid hormone suppression therapy in thyroid cancer and nodular goitre, and adjunct treatment in Hashimoto's thyroiditis.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Primary hypothyroidism: Start 25–50 mcg once daily; titrate by 12.5–25 mcg every 4–6 weeks
- Full replacement dose: 1.6 mcg/kg/day (typically 75–200 mcg/day)
- Elderly or cardiac patients: Start at 12.5–25 mcg once daily; titrate very slowly
- Thyroid cancer suppression: Higher doses to suppress TSH to <0.1 mIU/L

**When to Take**:
- **STRICTLY on an empty stomach, 30–60 minutes before breakfast**
- Consistency is critical — take at the same time daily
- Even small amounts of food, coffee, or other medications can reduce absorption by 20–40%
- If a dose is missed: take as soon as remembered on the same day; if next day, skip the missed dose — do NOT double dose

**Common Side Effects**:
- (At therapeutic doses, side effects are minimal — symptoms below indicate overtreatment/excess dose)
- Palpitations and tachycardia
- Tremors and anxiety
- Heat intolerance and excessive sweating
- Insomnia
- Weight loss
- Diarrhea

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Cardiac arrhythmias and Angina**: Excessive Levothyroxine dose can precipitate atrial fibrillation, angina, and myocardial infarction — especially in elderly patients and those with pre-existing heart disease.
- **Osteoporosis**: Long-term TSH suppression (in cancer therapy) is a risk factor for bone density loss, especially in post-menopausal women. Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA) monitoring recommended.
- **Adrenal insufficiency**: In patients with undiagnosed adrenal insufficiency, initiating Levothyroxine can precipitate adrenal crisis. Screen for adrenal insufficiency before starting treatment.
- **Do NOT switch brands arbitrarily** — bioavailability varies between Eltroxin, Thyronorm, and generic preparations. Switching brands requires TSH rechecking after 6 weeks.
- Symptoms of overdose (EMERGENCY): Chest pain, palpitations, severe tremors, confusion, high fever — seek immediate care.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Calcium carbonate**: Reduces Levothyroxine absorption by 20–40%. Separate by at least 4 hours.
- **Ferrous sulphate (Iron tablets)**: Reduces absorption significantly. Separate by at least 4 hours.
- **Antacids (Aluminium/Magnesium hydroxide), PPIs**: Reduce absorption. Take Levothyroxine at least 2 hours before or 4 hours after antacids.
- **Cholestyramine / Colestipol**: Binds Levothyroxine in gut; reduces absorption markedly. Separate by 4–6 hours.
- **Rifampicin, Carbamazepine, Phenytoin**: Induce hepatic metabolism of Levothyroxine; may require dose increase.
- **Warfarin**: Levothyroxine potentiates anticoagulant effect; INR may increase when thyroid function normalizes.
- **Sertraline / Antidepressants**: May increase Levothyroxine clearance; TSH monitoring required.
- **Soy products and high-fibre foods**: Reduce intestinal absorption; ensure consistent dietary patterns.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **Essential to continue and often dose needs to increase by 25–30% in 1st trimester**. Untreated hypothyroidism during pregnancy causes miscarriage, preterm birth, and fetal neurological damage. Immediate endocrinologist involvement required.
- **Lactation**: Safe. Excreted in breast milk in minimal amounts; does not affect infant thyroid function.

## Drug: Atorvastatin

**Primary Use**: HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor (Statin). Treatment of hypercholesterolaemia (high LDL cholesterol), hypertriglyceridaemia, mixed dyslipidaemia, and primary and secondary prevention of cardiovascular events (myocardial infarction, stroke) in high-risk patients.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Starting dose: 10–20 mg once daily
- Usual maintenance: 10–80 mg once daily
- High-intensity therapy (post-MI, high cardiovascular risk): 40–80 mg once daily
- Maximum dose: 80 mg once daily

**When to Take**:
- Once daily — can be taken at ANY time of day with or without food
- Preferably in the evening (cholesterol synthesis peaks at night), though this is less critical with Atorvastatin than older statins due to its longer half-life
- Consistency of timing matters more than the specific time

**Common Side Effects**:
- Myalgia (muscle ache/weakness) — most common complaint; affects ~5–10% of patients
- Headache
- Nausea and GI upset
- Elevated liver enzymes (AST/ALT) — usually mild and transient
- Nasopharyngitis
- Arthralgia (joint pain)

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Myopathy and Rhabdomyolysis**: Most serious adverse effect. Rhabdomyolysis = severe muscle breakdown → myoglobin released → acute kidney injury (dark/cola-coloured urine) → potentially fatal. EMERGENCY. Risk factors: high doses, CYP3A4 inhibitor co-administration, renal impairment, hypothyroidism, elderly, Asian patients (may require lower doses).
- **Symptoms requiring IMMEDIATE medical attention**: Unexplained severe muscle pain, weakness, tenderness, dark urine — STOP Atorvastatin and seek emergency care immediately.
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Clinically significant liver injury is rare but possible. Monitor LFTs at baseline and if symptomatic. Contraindicated in active liver disease.
- **New-onset Type 2 Diabetes**: Statins are associated with ~10% increased risk of T2DM in predisposed individuals. Does not outweigh cardiovascular benefits in most patients; monitor fasting glucose.
- **Statin intolerance** in Indian patients: Myalgia is more prevalent in South Asian populations; CK level monitoring helps distinguish true myopathy from benign myalgia.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Amlodipine**: Increases Atorvastatin AUC by ~35%. Limit Atorvastatin to 40 mg/day.
- **Clarithromycin / Erythromycin**: Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors; markedly increase Atorvastatin levels. Temporarily suspend statin or use minimum dose.
- **Ketoconazole / Itraconazole**: Potent CYP3A4 inhibitors; increase Atorvastatin levels 3–4-fold. Suspend statin during antifungal course.
- **Cyclosporine**: Dramatically increases statin levels; rhabdomyolysis risk. Atorvastatin generally contraindicated with Cyclosporine.
- **Gemfibrozil (Fibrate)**: Significantly increases rhabdomyolysis risk when combined with statins. If combination necessary, use Fenofibrate instead (safer than Gemfibrozil with statins).
- **Rifampicin**: Strong CYP3A4 inducer; substantially reduces Atorvastatin efficacy.
- **Colchicine**: Risk of myopathy when combined with high-dose statins.
- **Amiodarone**: Increases statin exposure; risk of myopathy.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **CONTRAINDICATED**. Statins are teratogenic in animal studies. Atherosclerosis is chronic; interrupting statin therapy during pregnancy is not harmful long-term. Discontinue immediately on confirmation of pregnancy.
- **Lactation**: **CONTRAINDICATED**. Excreted in breast milk; potential harm to infant.

## Drug: Rabeprazole

**Primary Use**: Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI). Treatment of GERD, peptic ulcer disease, H. pylori eradication (triple therapy), Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, and NSAID-associated ulcer prevention. Preferred over Pantoprazole in patients on Clopidogrel due to less CYP2C19 inhibition.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- GERD / Healing duodenal ulcer: 20 mg once daily for 4–8 weeks
- H. pylori eradication: 20 mg twice daily (with Amoxicillin 1g BD + Clarithromycin 500mg BD for 14 days)
- Maintenance GERD: 10–20 mg once daily
- Zollinger-Ellison: 60 mg once daily; may increase up to 100 mg/day

**When to Take**:
- **30–60 minutes BEFORE the first meal** (breakfast preferred)
- For twice-daily dosing: 30 minutes before breakfast and dinner
- Swallow whole — do NOT crush or chew enteric-coated tablets

**Common Side Effects**:
- Headache
- Diarrhea or constipation
- Nausea
- Abdominal pain
- Flatulence

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- Same long-term risks as all PPIs: Hypomagnesaemia, Hypocalcaemia, B12 deficiency, C. difficile risk, fracture risk.
- **Advantage over Pantoprazole/Omeprazole**: Rabeprazole has minimal CYP2C19 inhibitory activity, making it the preferred PPI in patients on **Clopidogrel** (antiplatelet therapy post-stenting/ACS).
- **Acute interstitial nephritis**: Rare. Symptoms: hematuria, oliguria, fever, rash. Seek emergency care.
- Do NOT use long-term without documented clinical indication.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Clopidogrel**: Unlike Omeprazole/Esomeprazole, Rabeprazole has minimal interaction — it is the PREFERRED PPI in cardiovascular patients on Clopidogrel.
- **Atazanavir**: Rabeprazole markedly reduces absorption of this HIV drug; combination contraindicated.
- **Methotrexate**: PPIs reduce renal clearance; methotrexate toxicity risk.
- **Ketoconazole**: Reduced absorption in alkaline gastric environment.
- **Digoxin**: Risk of increased Digoxin levels secondary to PPI-induced hypomagnesaemia.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Limited human data. Use only if clearly needed. Prefer Pantoprazole if a PPI is required.
- **Lactation**: Avoid; insufficient data.

## Drug: Azithromycin

**Primary Use**: Macrolide antibiotic. Treatment of community-acquired pneumonia (CAP), acute bacterial exacerbations of chronic bronchitis, pharyngitis, tonsillitis (penicillin-allergic patients), skin and soft tissue infections, sexually transmitted infections (Chlamydia, gonorrhoea — with Ceftriaxone), typhoid fever (pediatric and adult, in resistant strains), and non-tuberculous mycobacterial infections.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Community-acquired pneumonia: 500 mg once daily for 3–5 days
- Pharyngitis / Skin infections: 500 mg Day 1, then 250 mg Days 2–5
- Chlamydia (uncomplicated urogenital): 1 g single dose
- Typhoid (resistant cases): 1 g once daily for 5–7 days
- Pediatric: 10 mg/kg once daily for 3–5 days (maximum 500 mg/day)

**When to Take**:
- Can be taken with or without food
- Capsule formulations: avoid antacids within 2 hours
- For suspension: shake well before use
- Once daily dosing ensures excellent patient compliance
- **Complete the full course** even if symptoms resolve earlier

**Common Side Effects**:
- Nausea and vomiting (most common)
- Diarrhea and abdominal cramping
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Elevated liver enzymes
- Transient hearing disturbances (at high doses or prolonged courses)

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **QT Prolongation and Cardiac Arrhythmias**: Azithromycin prolongs cardiac QT interval, increasing risk of ventricular arrhythmias (Torsades de Pointes). EMERGENCY: symptoms include palpitations, syncope, or sudden cardiac death. Risk highest in: patients with pre-existing QT prolongation, electrolyte abnormalities (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia), or concurrent QT-prolonging drugs.
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Cholestatic jaundice and fulminant hepatic necrosis have been reported. Contraindicated in significant hepatic impairment.
- **Severe allergic reactions**: Anaphylaxis, Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS), DRESS syndrome — rare but life-threatening. Seek emergency care for skin blistering, mucous membrane involvement, or systemic hypersensitivity.
- **Antibiotic resistance**: Azithromycin resistance in Salmonella typhi is rising in India. Susceptibility testing mandatory before use in typhoid.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Antiarrhythmics (Amiodarone, Sotalol, Quinidine)**: Additive QT prolongation — potentially fatal arrhythmia. Contraindicated combination.
- **Fluoroquinolones (Moxifloxacin)**: Additive QT prolongation. Avoid concurrent use.
- **Haloperidol / Chlorpromazine / Quetiapine**: Additive QT prolongation.
- **Ergotamine / Dihydroergotamine**: Azithromycin inhibits CYP3A4; risk of ergotism (vasospasm, limb ischaemia).
- **Warfarin**: Potentiates anticoagulant effect; INR monitoring required.
- **Digoxin**: Azithromycin can increase Digoxin levels by altering gut flora (Eubacterium lentum); risk of Digoxin toxicity.
- **Antacids (Aluminium/Magnesium)**: Reduce peak Azithromycin levels; separate by 2 hours.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Category B. Generally considered acceptable for short courses when clinically indicated. Preferred over Tetracyclines for infections in pregnancy.
- **Lactation**: Excreted in breast milk. Short courses generally acceptable; monitor infant for GI symptoms.

## Drug: Diclofenac

**Primary Use**: Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID). Treatment of acute pain (musculoskeletal, dental, post-operative), dysmenorrhoea (painful menstruation), osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, acute gout, and renal colic. Available as oral tablets, injections, and topical gel in India.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Oral: 50 mg 2–3 times daily (maximum 150 mg/day)
- Modified-release (Diclofenac SR/Retard 100 mg): Once daily
- IM injection (acute pain): 75 mg once or twice daily (maximum 150 mg/day); NOT for more than 2 days IM
- Topical gel: Apply 2–4 g to affected area 3–4 times daily

**When to Take**:
- **With food or milk** — Diclofenac is one of the most gastroprotective NSAID choices but still causes GI irritation on empty stomach
- Take at a consistent time each day for chronic conditions
- Topical gel: for joint pain, apply directly over the affected joint

**Common Side Effects**:
- Nausea, dyspepsia, abdominal pain (very common)
- GI ulceration and bleeding (especially with prolonged use)
- Headache and dizziness
- Elevated liver enzymes (Diclofenac has the highest hepatotoxicity risk among common NSAIDs)
- Fluid retention and mild hypertension
- Skin reactions (rash, pruritus)

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **GI Bleeding and Perforation**: Peptic ulcer, perforation, and fatal GI bleeding can occur without warning, even in patients without prior GI history. Risk increased with age >65, concurrent Aspirin/Corticosteroid/Anticoagulant use, and H. pylori infection.
- **Cardiovascular Risk**: NSAIDs (especially Diclofenac) increase risk of MI and stroke — particularly with long-term use or in patients with existing cardiovascular disease. Diclofenac has the highest cardiovascular risk among oral NSAIDs.
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Diclofenac is the NSAID most associated with drug-induced liver injury. Monitor LFTs with long-term use. Contraindicated in severe hepatic disease.
- **Acute Kidney Injury**: NSAIDs reduce renal prostaglandin synthesis; risk of AKI with dehydration, in elderly, or pre-existing renal disease. Contraindicated in eGFR <30 mL/min.
- **Contraindicated in**: Active peptic ulcer, severe hepatic or renal impairment, heart failure, post-CABG surgery, established ischaemic heart disease or peripheral arterial disease.
- **Aspirin interaction**: Diclofenac competes with Aspirin for COX-1 binding, potentially reducing Aspirin's cardioprotective effect. If both required: take Aspirin 30–60 minutes before Diclofenac.
- **EMERGENCY signs of GI bleed**: Dark/tarry stools (melaena), vomiting blood, severe sudden abdominal pain — seek emergency care immediately.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Warfarin / Acenocoumarol**: NSAIDs potentiate anticoagulants; GI bleeding risk dramatically increased. Avoid concurrent use.
- **Aspirin (antiplatelet dose)**: Pharmacodynamic antagonism at COX-1. Risk of additive GI toxicity. If both needed, Aspirin must precede Diclofenac by 30–60 min.
- **ACE Inhibitors / ARBs / Diuretics**: The "Triple Whammy" combination (NSAID + ACE/ARB + Diuretic) causes acute kidney injury — common and dangerous combination in India.
- **Methotrexate**: NSAIDs reduce renal clearance of Methotrexate; severe bone marrow suppression and mucositis risk.
- **Cyclosporine / Tacrolimus**: NSAIDs increase nephrotoxicity risk.
- **SSRIs (Fluoxetine, Sertraline)**: Additive GI bleeding risk (SSRIs inhibit platelet function).
- **Lithium**: NSAIDs reduce lithium renal excretion; risk of lithium toxicity.
- **Quinolones (Ciprofloxacin)**: NSAIDs lower seizure threshold when combined with Quinolones.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **CONTRAINDICATED** in 3rd trimester — causes premature closure of ductus arteriosus and fetal renal impairment. Avoid in 1st trimester (miscarriage risk). Use with caution and minimum duration in 2nd trimester only.
- **Lactation**: Not recommended. Excreted in breast milk; risk of GI effects and platelet dysfunction in infant.

## Drug: Losartan

**Primary Use**: Angiotensin II Receptor Blocker (ARB). Treatment of hypertension, nephroprotection in Type 2 diabetic nephropathy (proteinuric renal disease), and heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF). Also used for hypertension in patients intolerant to ACE inhibitors (due to cough).

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Hypertension: 50 mg once daily; may increase to 100 mg once daily
- Diabetic nephropathy: 50 mg once daily, increasing to 100 mg once daily based on BP response
- Heart failure: 12.5 mg once daily; titrate up to 150 mg once daily
- Hepatic impairment: Start at 25 mg once daily (Losartan is extensively hepatically metabolized)

**When to Take**:
- Once daily, with or without food
- Fixed time each day for consistency
- No special dietary restrictions

**Common Side Effects**:
- Dizziness and light-headedness (first-dose hypotension)
- Upper respiratory symptoms
- Back pain
- Hyperkalaemia (elevated potassium) — especially in renal impairment
- Fatigue
- Mild oedema
- (Unlike ACE inhibitors, Losartan does NOT cause dry cough — a key differentiator in clinical practice)

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Pregnancy**: **CONTRAINDICATED** from 2nd trimester. Causes fetal renal agenesis, oligohydramnios, skull hypoplasia, neonatal hypotension, renal failure, and death. Discontinue immediately on confirmed pregnancy.
- **Hyperkalaemia**: Life-threatening if severe. Risk increased with: diabetes, renal impairment, concurrent K-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs, ACE inhibitor use. ECG changes include peaked T-waves, wide QRS, arrhythmia. EMERGENCY.
- **Bilateral renal artery stenosis**: Can precipitate acute renal failure. Contraindicated.
- **Volume depletion**: First dose in volume-depleted patients can cause severe hypotension. Correct dehydration before initiating.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **ACE Inhibitors (Ramipril, Enalapril)**: Dual RAAS blockade — significantly increased risk of hypotension, hyperkalaemia, and acute renal failure. Generally contraindicated.
- **Aliskiren**: Triple RAAS blockade. Contraindicated in diabetes and renal impairment.
- **Potassium-sparing diuretics (Spironolactone, Amiloride, Triamterene)**: Additive hyperkalaemia. Life-threatening arrhythmia risk.
- **NSAIDs (Diclofenac, Ibuprofen, Naproxen)**: Attenuate antihypertensive effect and increase AKI risk. The "Triple Whammy" — Losartan + NSAID + Diuretic = acute renal failure risk.
- **Lithium**: ARBs increase lithium levels; toxicity risk.
- **Fluconazole**: Inhibits CYP2C9; impairs conversion of Losartan to its active metabolite (E3174), reducing efficacy.
- **Rifampicin**: Reduces Losartan plasma levels via CYP induction.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** — discontinue immediately.
- **Lactation**: Avoid. Insufficient safety data.

## Drug: Levocetirizine

**Primary Use**: R-enantiomer of Cetirizine; third-generation H1 antihistamine. Treatment of allergic rhinitis (seasonal and perennial), chronic idiopathic urticaria (CIU), atopic dermatitis, and insect bite reactions. Preferred over Cetirizine in patients needing lower sedation risk.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Allergic rhinitis / Urticaria: 5 mg once daily
- Elderly or renal impairment (CrCl 30–50 mL/min): 2.5 mg once daily
- Renal impairment (CrCl 10–30 mL/min): 2.5 mg every 48 hours
- Children 6–11 years: 2.5 mg once daily
- Children 2–5 years: 1.25 mg once daily

**When to Take**:
- Once daily, preferably in the evening
- Can be taken with or without food
- Consistent daily timing recommended for chronic allergy management

**Common Side Effects**:
- Somnolence (less than Cetirizine; affects ~6% of users)
- Dry mouth
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Nasopharyngitis

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Renal impairment**: Levocetirizine is primarily renally excreted. Mandatory dose reduction in renal impairment. Contraindicated in severe renal failure (CrCl <10 mL/min) and in patients undergoing haemodialysis.
- **CNS depression**: Despite lower sedation profile than Chlorphenamine (Chlorpheniramine), Levocetirizine can still impair driving and machinery operation in susceptible individuals.
- Not recommended in children under 2 years of age.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **CNS depressants (Alcohol, Benzodiazepines, Opioids)**: Additive sedation.
- **Ritonavir**: Increases Levocetirizine exposure; enhanced CNS side effects.
- **Theophylline (high dose)**: May reduce clearance of Levocetirizine.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: Limited human data. Avoid in 1st trimester. Use in 2nd/3rd trimester only if benefits clearly outweigh risks. Consult allergist/obstetrician.
- **Lactation**: Not recommended. May cause infant sedation.

## Drug: Glimepiride

**Primary Use**: Second-generation sulphonylurea oral antidiabetic drug. Treatment of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) as monotherapy or in combination with Metformin or Insulin when dietary modifications and exercise are insufficient. Stimulates pancreatic beta-cell insulin secretion.

**Standard Dosage (Adult)**:
- Starting dose: 1 mg once daily
- Usual maintenance: 1–4 mg once daily
- Maximum dose: 8 mg once daily (higher doses rarely add benefit)
- In renal impairment (mild-moderate): 1 mg once daily; use with extreme caution
- Elderly patients: Start at 1 mg; strict hypoglycaemia monitoring essential

**When to Take**:
- **With the first main meal of the day** (breakfast in most contexts) — synchronizes drug action with meal-induced glucose rise
- **NEVER take on an empty stomach** — risk of hypoglycaemia
- Take at a consistent time daily
- If a meal is delayed or skipped, do NOT take the dose — risk of severe hypoglycaemia

**Common Side Effects**:
- **Hypoglycaemia** (most common and most dangerous — see CRITICAL WARNINGS)
- Weight gain (insulin secretagogue effect)
- Nausea and GI upset
- Dizziness and headache
- Mild skin rash or photosensitivity

**CRITICAL WARNINGS**:
- **Hypoglycaemia** (MEDICAL EMERGENCY): Blood glucose <70 mg/dL. Mild-moderate: trembling, sweating, palpitations, hunger, confusion. Severe: seizures, loss of consciousness, coma. Treatment: 15 g of fast-acting carbohydrate (3–4 glucose tablets, 150 mL fruit juice, 1 tablespoon sugar dissolved in water). If unconscious: IV Dextrose 50% 50 mL (hospital) or Glucagon IM 1 mg. Seek emergency care immediately.
- **Severe hypoglycaemia risk with**: Missed meals, excessive alcohol, strenuous exercise, concurrent use of other antidiabetics, renal/hepatic impairment, elderly patients.
- **Contraindicated in**: Type 1 DM, diabetic ketoacidosis, severe hepatic impairment, severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min).
- **Sulphonylurea allergy**: Cross-reactivity possible with sulfonamide antibiotics.
- **Perioperative period**: Generally held 24–48 hours before major surgery; switch to IV insulin infusion.
- **SIADH**: Sulphonylureas can rarely cause hyponatraemia.

**Severe Drug Interactions**:
- **Fluconazole / Miconazole**: CYP2C9 inhibitors; markedly increase Glimepiride plasma levels. Severe, prolonged hypoglycaemia risk. Avoid combination.
- **NSAIDs (especially high-dose Aspirin)**: Displace sulphonylureas from protein binding; potentiate hypoglycaemic effect.
- **Warfarin**: Glimepiride may potentiate anticoagulant effect. INR monitoring required.
- **Beta-blockers (Atenolol, Propranolol)**: Mask tachycardia (sympathetic response) of hypoglycaemia, making it harder to detect. Sweating is not masked — educate patient.
- **Alcohol**: Inhibits hepatic gluconeogenesis; severe and prolonged hypoglycaemia risk.
- **ACE Inhibitors**: May potentiate hypoglycaemic effect of Glimepiride via enhanced insulin sensitivity.
- **Rifampicin**: CYP2C9 inducer; reduces Glimepiride efficacy; may need dose increase.
- **Corticosteroids (Dexamethasone, Prednisolone)**: Antagonise hypoglycaemic effect; significantly raise blood glucose.
- **Thiazide diuretics**: Reduce glucose tolerance; antagonise sulphonylurea effect.

**Pregnancy/Lactation Safety**:
- **Pregnancy**: **CONTRAINDICATED**. Sulphonylureas cross the placenta; cause neonatal hypoglycaemia. **Insulin is the standard of care** for diabetes in pregnancy in India.
- **Lactation**: **CONTRAINDICATED**. Excreted in breast milk; risk of neonatal hypoglycaemia.

# General Health & Vitals Guidelines

## Vitals: Blood Pressure

**Classification Source**: JNC 8 / ESC 2018 Guidelines (as adopted in Indian clinical practice)

**Normal (Optimal)**: Systolic <120 mmHg AND Diastolic <80 mmHg
**Normal (Acceptable)**: Systolic 120–129 mmHg AND Diastolic <80 mmHg
**Elevated (Prehypertension)**: Systolic 130–139 mmHg OR Diastolic 80–89 mmHg
**Hypertension Stage 1**: Systolic 140–159 mmHg OR Diastolic 90–99 mmHg
**Hypertension Stage 2**: Systolic ≥160 mmHg OR Diastolic ≥100 mmHg
**Hypertensive Urgency**: Systolic >180 mmHg OR Diastolic >110–120 mmHg — WITHOUT end-organ damage; requires urgent GP/physician evaluation within hours
**Hypertensive Crisis (EMERGENCY)**: Systolic >180 mmHg OR Diastolic >120 mmHg — WITH signs of end-organ damage (chest pain, severe headache, blurred vision, confusion, shortness of breath, neurological deficits); requires IMMEDIATE emergency department attendance

**Measurement Standards**:
- Measure after 5 minutes of seated rest
- Both arms measured at first visit; use higher-reading arm for follow-up
- Two readings separated by 1–2 minutes; average of the two used
- Avoid caffeine, exercise, and smoking within 30 minutes of measurement

**Hypotension**: Systolic <90 mmHg OR Diastolic <60 mmHg
- **Orthostatic (Postural) Hypotension**: Drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing — common in elderly, diabetics, and patients on antihypertensives

**Indian-Specific Context**:
- Indian patients tend to have higher rates of isolated systolic hypertension and salt-sensitive hypertension
- White coat hypertension (elevated in clinic, normal at home) is prevalent; home BP monitoring is recommended
- Target BP in diabetes: <130/80 mmHg (per ADA/Indian Diabetes Council guidelines)
- Target BP in CKD with proteinuria: <130/80 mmHg

## Vitals: Heart Rate (Pulse)

**Classification Source**: AHA / Indian Cardiology Society Guidelines

**Normal Resting Heart Rate (Adult)**: 60–100 beats per minute (bpm)
**Bradycardia**: <60 bpm
**Tachycardia**: >100 bpm
**Well-trained athletes**: Resting HR of 40–60 bpm is normal and not pathological
**Children (1–11 years)**: Normal HR 70–110 bpm
**Newborns (0–1 month)**: Normal HR 100–160 bpm

**Bradycardia Significance**:
- Asymptomatic bradycardia in athletes: Usually benign (vagal tone)
- Symptomatic bradycardia (dizziness, syncope, dyspnoea, chest pain): Requires urgent evaluation
- Causes in Indian context: Hypothyroidism, beta-blocker overdose, complete heart block, inferior MI, electrolyte imbalance (hyperkalaemia)

**Tachycardia Significance**:
- Sinus tachycardia (HR 100–150 bpm): Usually secondary to fever, pain, anxiety, dehydration, anaemia, hyperthyroidism — treat the cause
- Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT, HR 150–250 bpm): Paroxysmal, regular — requires ECG evaluation
- Ventricular tachycardia (VT, HR >150 bpm with wide QRS on ECG): EMERGENCY — risk of ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death
- Atrial Fibrillation (AF): Irregularly irregular rhythm; HR variable — requires ECG diagnosis and anticoagulation assessment

**EMERGENCY Heart Rate Triggers** (Seek immediate emergency care):
- HR >150 bpm with symptoms (chest pain, dizziness, syncope, dyspnoea)
- HR <40 bpm with symptoms
- Irregular rhythm with haemodynamic instability
- Any new palpitations with pre-syncope or syncope

## Vitals: SpO2 (Oxygen Saturation / Pulse Oximetry)

**Classification Source**: BTS / AIIMS / WHO Oxygen Therapy Guidelines

**Normal SpO2**: 95–100%
**Acceptable Lower Limit (COPD patients)**: 88–92% (higher SpO2 targets are inappropriate in chronic hypercapnic COPD — risk of suppressing hypoxic respiratory drive)
**Hypoxemia (Requires Prompt Medical Attention)**: SpO2 90–94%
**Moderate Hypoxemia (URGENT)**: SpO2 85–89% — supplemental oxygen required; urgent physician evaluation
**Severe Hypoxemia (EMERGENCY)**: SpO2 <85% — life-threatening; immediate emergency care and oxygen therapy required
**Critical (EMERGENCY)**: SpO2 <80% — risk of cardiac arrhythmia, organ failure, and death

**Measurement Accuracy Considerations**:
- SpO2 may be falsely HIGH in carbon monoxide poisoning (pulse oximeter cannot distinguish oxyhaemoglobin from carboxyhaemoglobin)
- SpO2 may read falsely LOW with: nail polish (especially dark), poor peripheral circulation, hypothermia, excessive movement, dark skin pigmentation (some device-specific bias reported)
- SpO2 should always be correlated with clinical presentation — a patient who is breathless at SpO2 95% needs evaluation regardless

**Indian-Specific Context**:
- SpO2 monitoring became mainstream during COVID-19 pandemic in India
- SpO2 <94% in a COVID-19 patient was the threshold for hospital admission (AIIMS/ICMR 2020–2022 guidelines)
- Anaemia can cause symptoms of hypoxia despite normal SpO2 — total oxygen-carrying capacity is reduced even if haemoglobin is fully saturated

## Vitals: Blood Glucose

**Classification Source**: ADA 2024 / RSSDI (Research Society for the Study of Diabetes in India) Guidelines

**Fasting Blood Glucose (FBG)** (8 hours fasting, venous plasma):
- Normal: 70–99 mg/dL
- Impaired Fasting Glucose (Pre-diabetes): 100–125 mg/dL
- Diabetes Mellitus: ≥126 mg/dL (confirmed on two separate occasions)

**Post-Prandial Blood Glucose (PPBG)** (2 hours after meal):
- Normal: <140 mg/dL
- Impaired Glucose Tolerance (Pre-diabetes): 140–199 mg/dL
- Diabetes Mellitus: ≥200 mg/dL

**Random Blood Glucose (RBG)**:
- Diabetes Mellitus (symptomatic): ≥200 mg/dL + symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss)

**HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin)**:
- Normal: <5.7%
- Pre-diabetes: 5.7–6.4%
- Diabetes: ≥6.5%
- Target in managed T2DM (general): <7.0%
- Target in elderly/comorbid patients: <7.5–8.0%
- Target in pregnancy (gestational diabetes): <6.0%

**Hypoglycaemia Thresholds**:
- Alert value: <70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L) — treat regardless of symptoms
- Clinically significant: <54 mg/dL (3.0 mmol/L)
- Severe hypoglycaemia: <40 mg/dL OR any hypoglycaemia requiring external assistance

**EMERGENCY Glucose Values**:
- Severe hypoglycaemia with altered consciousness: Treat with IV Dextrose immediately
- Blood glucose >500 mg/dL: Suspect Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS) or DKA — requires emergency hospitalization
- Blood glucose >300 mg/dL with ketones, vomiting, abdominal pain: Suspect DKA — EMERGENCY

## Vitals: Body Temperature

**Normal Oral Temperature (Adult)**: 36.1–37.2°C (97.0–99.0°F)
**Low-grade Fever**: 37.3–38.0°C (99.1–100.4°F)
**Fever**: >38.0°C (100.4°F)
**High Fever (Requires Attention)**: >39.0°C (102.2°F)
**Hyperpyrexia (EMERGENCY)**: >40.0°C (104.0°F) — risk of febrile seizures in children; heat stroke in adults
**Hypothermia**: <35.0°C (95.0°F) — EMERGENCY in severe cases (<32°C)

**Indian-Specific Context**:
- Fever workup priority in India: Malaria (especially in endemic states — Odisha, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh), Dengue, Typhoid, Leptospirosis, Chikungunya
- Dengue warning signs (EMERGENCY): Abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, liver enlargement, rising haematocrit with rapidly decreasing platelet count

## Vitals: Respiratory Rate

**Normal Respiratory Rate (Adult)**: 12–20 breaths per minute (bpm)
**Tachypnoea**: >20 breaths per minute
**Bradypnoea**: <12 breaths per minute
**EMERGENCY (Respiratory distress)**: >30 breaths per minute

**EMERGENCY Triggers**:
- RR >30/min with SpO2 <94% and fever: Suspect severe pneumonia or COVID-19 — emergency hospitalization
- RR <8/min: Risk of respiratory failure — may indicate opioid overdose, CNS depression, or metabolic alkalosis

## General Drug Interaction Principles (Indian Clinical Context)

**The "Triple Whammy" Combination** (AVOID):
- ACE inhibitor or ARB + NSAID + Diuretic = High risk of acute kidney injury (AKI)
- Common dangerous combination in Indian patients with hypertension taking Diclofenac for pain

**High-Risk Antibiotic Combinations for QT Prolongation**:
- Azithromycin + Fluoroquinolone (Moxifloxacin/Levofloxacin) = CONTRAINDICATED
- Azithromycin + Antipsychotics (Haloperidol, Chlorpromazine) = CONTRAINDICATED

**NSAIDs Contraindicated Post-Cardiac Stent**:
- Patients on Aspirin + Clopidogrel (dual antiplatelet): NSAID use dramatically increases bleeding risk; Paracetamol preferred for pain

**Antibiotic Stewardship (India-Specific)**:
- India is one of the world's largest consumers of antibiotics
- Azithromycin, Amoxicillin, and Ciprofloxacin resistance rates are rising significantly in community-acquired infections
- Blood culture and sensitivity testing before initiating antibiotics in hospitalized patients is mandatory
- Do NOT prescribe antibiotics for viral URTI, uncomplicated influenza, or COVID-19 (without confirmed bacterial co-infection)

## Emergency Reference: When to Go to Hospital IMMEDIATELY

**Seek Emergency Care (Call 108 or go to nearest casualty) for**:
- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness lasting >5 minutes
- Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap" — worst headache of life)
- One-sided weakness, facial drooping, slurred speech (stroke symptoms — Act FAST)
- SpO2 <90% or severe breathlessness at rest
- Severe hypoglycaemia with unconsciousness
- Severe allergic reaction: urticaria + throat swelling + difficulty breathing (anaphylaxis)
- Suspected drug overdose (especially Paracetamol, Digoxin, antidiabetics)
- Vomiting blood or passing black tarry stools
- Seizure lasting >5 minutes or first-ever seizure
- Blood pressure >180/120 mmHg with headache, visual disturbance, or chest pain
- Fever >40°C in a child or adult with altered consciousness
- Suspected medication error in children involving sulphonylureas, anticoagulants, or cardiac drugs
